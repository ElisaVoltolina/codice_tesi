## Introduction

In my thesis work, we address a transportation problem arising from a real-world medical application, specifically the non-emergency transportation of patients. 
Non-emergency patient transportation represents an essential service that ensures mobility and access to healthcare facilities for patients who are unable to travel independently. This type of service typically includes the transportation of patients to hospital clinics for medical treatments, diagnostic examinations, or therapies, as well as transfers between healthcare facilities.
In this context, a transportation service consists of moving a patient from a pickup location (e.g., the patient’s home or hospital) to a delivery location (e.g., a healthcare facility or the patient’s home) and performing the return trip.
The problem studied in this work belongs to the class of Vehicle RoutingProblems (VRP), a family of optimization problems that aims to design optimal routes for a fleet of vehicles serving a set of customers under specific
operational constraints. 
From a modeling perspective, the transportation service analyzed in this work can be formulated as a static Dial-a-Ride Problem with Time Windows (DARPTW). This class of problems has been extensively studied in the field of operations research and transportation systems, as it captures the key operational characteristics of patient transportation: adherence to predefined pickup and delivery times, the requirement to ensure round trips, and the consideration of vehicle capacity and service time constraints.   
In particular, the formulation proposed in this thesis is inspired by the Selective Dial-a-Ride Problem (SDARP). This modeling approach is
especially suitable for our context, where resource limitations, such as the availability of a single vehicle, make it unrealistic to assume that all transportation requests can always be fulfilled. The selective variant of the DARP explicitly incorporates this operational limitation by allowing only a subset of total requests to be served, while the remaining ones may left unassigned. The objective function in such models typically aims to maximize the number of served requests or to minimize a weighted combination of unmet demand
and total operational costs. Therefore, the Selective DARPTW frame-
work provides a rigorous and flexible modeling foundation that captures both the logistical aspects (vehicle capacity, time windows, travel times) and the service quality considerations (minimizing patient waiting times and ensuring timely arrivals to medical appointments). This formulation is particularly suitable for healthcare transportation systems, where operational efficiency
must be carefully balanced with fairness, patient satisfaction, and the overall quality of service.      
To address this problem, we have developed a two solution approach com-
bining exact and heuristic methodologies. The problem is first formulated as a Mixed Integer Linear Programming (MILP) model, which we solve using the Gurobi optimization solver. To complement this, we have designed a constructive heuristic algorithm based on sequential insertion techniques.
